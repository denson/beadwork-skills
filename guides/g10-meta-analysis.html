beadwork · meta-analysis

Have one agent check the others' work.

Agents make mistakes — they drift off a convention, apply different standards to similar tasks, miss what a peer just flagged. The cheap way to catch it is to point another agent at the work itself: the recorded decisions and verdicts, not the chat that produced them. A chat log can't be audited well — it's one long stream, the speaker is implicit, the timing is muddy, most of it is noise. Beadwork's records can: every entry is signed, timestamped, and structured. That's the substrate an auditing agent needs.

The audit agent doesn't watch the others work — it reads what they wrote afterward, over one project or across many at once, and asks questions no single agent can answer about its own work: Did two agents apply the same criteria across a dozen similar tasks? Has a convention drifted over the quarter? Did a later note quietly contradict an earlier one?

An engineer's review pipeline

Three review agents — security, style, test-coverage — each post a verdict on the same change. A fourth, reading all three, flags it: "security wants strict CORS headers; style wants them stripped — these contradict."

The conflict surfaces before it ships, not after.

A data analyst's pipeline

A cleaner, then a tester, then a visualizer — each writes its step to the record. An auditing agent reads the chain: "the tester ran on the full population, but the cleaner had filtered to active users — that's a gap."

A subtlety a chat log would bury.

A lawyer across many cases

An audit agent reads a year of document-review records spanning many matters and checks whether privilege calls were made consistently from one agent to the next — the kind of uniformity that's hard to eyeball across that much work.

Consistency across cases, which only a cross-case read catches.

The auditor is just another agent, reading the substrate every prior agent produced — over one project or across many. What makes it work is that the records are signed, timestamped, and structured; a chat history isn't.

And the honest flip side: if your work is a single short session with nothing to cross-check, there's nothing to meta-analyze. This earns its place once several agents or sessions have left a record worth auditing.
