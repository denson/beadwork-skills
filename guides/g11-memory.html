beadwork · durable memory

Memory that actually survives.

Most of what your AI needs across time isn't the whole conversation — it's a few specific things that have to survive: the decision you reached, the constraint you hit, the blocker you noted, the half-finished plan. Beadwork is where your assistant writes those down, so the next session — next week, next machine, even a different AI — picks them up instead of starting blank.

Those notes survive a lot: the chat ending, a context reset, an app restart, switching computers, switching AI vendors, and plain time. And because the record keeps the whole timeline, you can see how you got somewhere — the path, not just the latest state. Most "AI memory" tools keep only the current state; this keeps the trail.

An hour, not three weeks

A lawyer's case, picked up mid-stream

A matter opens in March and settles in November. Someone new joins in September; their assistant reads the case record from the start — discovery, depositions, the decisions made — and they're up to speed in an hour instead of three weeks.

Over a week

An analyst's investigation writes its own memo

A metric anomaly takes a week to chase. Each day's findings go into the record. On day seven, the assistant writes the stakeholder memo straight from that trail — grounded in what actually happened, not reconstructed from a fading chat.

Across engagements

A solo founder's lessons carry forward

A client engagement in spring, a similar one in fall. The assistant surfaces the relevant prior lessons — "you tried this in spring; here's what worked and what didn't" — instead of relearning them.

The honest flip side: if you only ever have one short conversation, there's nothing to carry forward. This earns its place when context needs to outlive the chat — across days, machines, or people.
