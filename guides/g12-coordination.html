beadwork · coordination

A team that shares one workspace.

Run more than one AI session, or work with other people, and beadwork becomes the shared place they all read and write. Your planning session, your building session, and your review session build on each other's work — a small team of specialist assistants instead of one amnesiac generalist. Most often they're sessions of the same model in different roles; the same mechanism works across vendors too, if you ever mix them.

Within one job

An engineer's review

Three review assistants — security, style, test-coverage — each post a verdict to the same item. The submitter's assistant reads the consolidated result and synthesizes one response.

Across days

An analysis pipeline

Monday's cleaner notes the cleaned data; Tuesday's tester reads that and runs the right tests; Wednesday's visualizer reads both and makes the charts. Each step's input is the last step's note.

Across teams

A support escalation

A customer issue opens an item; engineering's assistant posts a fix ETA; the support manager's assistant reads it and drafts the customer reply. Three departments, no Slack thread to chase.

Across organizations

A warehouse and its vendor

A logistics partner's assistant posts pickup schedules to a shared item; the warehouse manager's confirms them or proposes alternatives. Two organizations coordinating on their own time, with a full record and no shared chat to maintain.

It's conflict-free by construction: different items are different files, so two assistants writing at once don't collide — no merge mess, no coordination overhead.

The honest flip side: if you work alone in one session, you don't need any of this. It earns its place the moment a second assistant or a second person needs the same context. For how the shared record works, see why "it's just git" matters.
