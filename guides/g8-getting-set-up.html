Getting set up — what to expect.

Setting beadwork up is a handful of one-time steps, and your assistant does the actual work — you just approve a couple of things along the way. Here's what to expect, so nothing catches you off guard.

What your assistant does

  1. Installs git, if your computer doesn't already have it.
  2. Installs beadwork — a small program.
  3. Makes a folder for your notes.
  4. Connects a cloud account — only if you chose backup or sharing back in "where your data lives."

You okay each step as it comes; your assistant runs it.

On Windows, expect a security prompt or two

When a new tool is installed or run for the first time, Windows usually shows a warning — a "Windows protected your PC" box, or "Do you want to allow this app to make changes?" That's Windows being careful about a tool it hasn't seen before, not a sign anything's wrong.

And it isn't a leap of faith. Git is one of the most widely used tools in all of software — it's on the machines of the large majority of programmers worldwide. And beadwork is a fully open project: your assistant can read its actual source and confirm it's the genuine, safe thing before it installs anything.

Your assistant will tell you which prompt is coming and that it's safe to allow — you click through, and you're done. (On a Mac it's similar: you may be asked for your login password once, or to confirm opening the program.)

What you're allowing

The whole permission surface is small: install git (if it's missing), install beadwork, make a notes folder, and read and write inside it. Beadwork can't make purchases, send messages, or touch anything outside that folder.

If a step balks

Setup is where the occasional hiccup lives. If something stalls — a step that errors, a prompt you didn't expect, a download that didn't land — just tell your assistant what you saw. It can almost always sort it out, and the steps are reversible. Once you're past setup, trouble is rare: working on your own you won't hit the kind of conflicts teams do, and nothing is ever silently lost.

All told it's a few minutes, mostly your assistant working. The friction, when there's any, is your computer being cautious — not beadwork being broken.
