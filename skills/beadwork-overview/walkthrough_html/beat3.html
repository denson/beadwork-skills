Where this kind of memory helps.

Same tool, very different jobs. A small sample of where the "AI that remembers, AIs that share notes, teams that coordinate through their AIs" pattern earns its keep.

Same interaction in every case.

Seven snapshots

  • Software engineer — today's session reads yesterday's debugging notes; PR review agents (security, style, tests) all write verdicts to one ticket; a synthesizer agent reads them.
  • Data scientist — a week-long investigation kept across five sessions; a methodology auditor reads the chain six months later when leadership asks "why did you conclude X?"
  • Logistics analyst — vendor scorecards and route-model iterations logged so abandoned approaches stay explainable.
  • Warehouse manager — day-shift supervisor's AI logs an incident; night-shift supervisor's AI reads it on session start; vendor at the logistics partner reads/writes from their end of the same shared shelf.
  • Customer support manager — an escalation opens a ticket; engineering's AI posts a fix ETA on it; the manager's AI reads the update and drafts the customer reply. Three departments, no email chain.
  • Lawyer — privilege determinations preserved with timestamp, drafter, and reasoning. Opposing counsel challenges a redaction nine months later; bw show produces the original reasoning.
  • Solo entrepreneur — every business decision (which tool, which vendor, which contractor) logged with rationale. Tax-time arrives; the AI runs bw show on the year's expense decisions and formats for the accountant.

The interaction is identical in every case: you talk to your AI; the AI uses bw on your behalf. What differs is what each role tracks — not how they interact with the tool.

And in the team cases, the same shelf serves multiple humans plus their AIs — not because anything special is added, but because git was already designed for many-people-sharing-one-thing.
